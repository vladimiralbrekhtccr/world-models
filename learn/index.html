1 — The big idea

A 3D scene you can fly through, built from millions of fuzzy coloured blobs.

You have some photos of a place. You want to render it from camera angles you never photographed — fly through it, orbit it, walk in. That problem is called novel view synthesis.

Before 3D Gaussian Splatting, two main answers existed:

  • Textured mesh. Build a polygon model from the photos and paint colours on it. Great for solid objects, terrible for foliage, hair, smoke, glass — anything fuzzy or transparent.
  • NeRF (2020). Train a neural network that, when you ask "what's at point (x,y,z) when looking in this direction?", answers with a colour and a density. Render by asking it millions of times per frame. Photorealistic — but originally minutes per image.

3DGS (2023) flipped the architecture. Instead of one neural network that you query, the scene is just a literal list of millions of fuzzy 3D blobs. To render you project them to the screen and blend. No neural network runs at render time.

The key shift, in one line: NeRF is a calculator, 3DGS is a spreadsheet.
A calculator doesn't store 2+2=4 anywhere — you ask, it computes. A spreadsheet has 4 sitting in a cell — you just read it.
NeRF computes every pixel by asking a network "what's at this point?". 3DGS already has the answer written down as millions of blobs.

Run the widget below to feel the difference. Both panels paint the same picture, but the left one computes each pixel (NeRF style) and the right one reads them all at once (3DGS style).

In real life NeRF takes seconds, 3DGS milliseconds. The animation here is exaggerated for clarity. Same idea: one computes, the other reads.

The payoffs of being a spreadsheet:

  • Real-time rendering. 60–120 FPS, vs NeRF's seconds.
  • Works in a browser. Reading data + blending is cheap; running a neural network on every pixel is not.
  • Easier tooling. A list of blobs can be streamed, edited, compressed, or imported into Blender. A neural net can't.

And two facts that fall out for free:

  • 3DGS files are big — a spreadsheet of millions of rows is big. (NeRF's network file is small, but you pay for it on every render.)
  • One file per scene. Every scene gets its own spreadsheet.

2 — The Gaussian

The atom of a 3DGS scene. Position, shape, opacity, colour — about seventeen numbers, and that's it.

A 3D Gaussian is one fuzzy, semi-transparent, coloured ellipsoid. Picture a soft glowing blob that's solid in the middle and fades smoothly to nothing at the edge. A scene is millions of these, overlapping.

Each blob stores a few numbers:

PropertyNumbersMeaning
position (mean)3where the centre sits in 3D
scale3how long it is along its 3 local axes
rotation (quaternion)4how the ellipsoid is oriented
opacity1how solid vs see-through
colour (spherical harmonics)3–48flat colour, or colour that changes with viewing angle

Open the widget below. The numbers in the readout panel are literally what one row of a `.ply` file stores for one Gaussian. Squash scale Z to near zero and you get a flat disc — the "splat" that tiles a wall. That's the key trick: each Gaussian stretches independently along three axes to fit whatever shape it's covering. (The fancy word is anisotropy; the boring phrase is "ellipsoids, not spheres.")

drag to orbit · this is the data a `.ply` row stores for one Gaussian

Colour can change with viewing angle

A matte wall looks the same colour from every angle. A glossy apple has a white highlight that moves as you walk around it. To capture that, a Gaussian's colour isn't one fixed RGB — it's a small function of viewing direction, encoded as spherical harmonics (SH) coefficients:

  • SH degree 0 = 3 numbers = one flat colour.
  • SH degree 3 = 48 numbers = colour shifts with angle (captures highlights, sheen).

The widget below shows the same blob two ways. In flat mode the colour never changes as you orbit. In view-dependent mode it shifts (exaggerated for teaching). Real SH is more subtle, but the principle is identical: the colour stored on each Gaussian is a tiny function, not a constant.

widget · flat vs view-dependent colour
drag to orbit and watch the colour. Splatfacto outputs SH-3; our Lyra and WorldMirror outputs are SH-0 (flat).

3 — Building a scene

From a folder of photos to millions of fitted Gaussians, via "differentiable rasterization" (a renderer that runs backwards).

Classical 3DGS builds a scene by per-scene optimization — each scene gets its own little training run. Roughly:

Step 1 — recover the cameras (COLMAP / SfM)

Before any Gaussians, you must know where each photo was taken. COLMAP detects features in every photo, matches them, and solves for each camera's 3D position and orientation. As a by-product it gives you a sparse 3D point cloud — a few thousand confident 3D points.

Step 2 — initialise the blobs

Put one Gaussian at each COLMAP point. Maybe 100k blobs. Random sizes, rough colours. This is the seed.

Step 3 — the training loop, repeated ~30,000 times

  1. Pick a training photo (whose camera you know).
  2. Render the current Gaussians from that camera → predicted image.
  3. Compare predicted vs real photo → a single number called the loss.
  4. Backpropagate — because the renderer is differentiable, you can compute, for every Gaussian, which way to nudge it to make the next render less wrong.
  5. Update every Gaussian by that nudge.

"Differentiable" sounds scary; it just means the renderer also runs backwards. A normal renderer goes scene → image. A differentiable one also tells you, given "the image was wrong here", which Gaussian to blame and by how much. That backward signal is what makes optimization possible at all.

Step 4 — adaptive density control

While training, the optimiser also adjusts how many Gaussians exist:

  • Clone / split Gaussians where the picture is still blurry.
  • Prune Gaussians that have gone transparent or absurdly large.

So the count grows from ~100k to several million, concentrated where the scene needs detail. Total wall time: minutes to an hour on one GPU.

The catch: per-scene. The result is one specific scene; nothing is reused for the next one. And you need photos covering every important angle — gaps become holes or smears. Module 6 covers newer methods that escape this.

The plant demo is exactly this pipeline: phone video → frames → COLMAP → 30k iterations → splat. ~30 minutes end-to-end on one GPU.

4 — Rendering in the browser

Project, sort, blend. Three steps, no neural network, GPU-friendly — which is why it runs at 60 FPS in a tab.

For every frame, for every Gaussian:

  1. Project its 3D ellipsoid onto the screen. A 3D ellipsoid projects to a 2D ellipse — a "splat".
  2. Sort all splats by depth (far → near).
  3. Blend them front-to-back: each splat contributes colour × opacity × its 2D Gaussian falloff, accumulated until the pixel is opaque.

That's it. Project, sort, blend. No ray-marching, no neural network. This is "rasterization" — the work GPUs were built for — which is why it's real-time where NeRF's per-pixel network queries were not.

The only fiddly part is the sort: it has to be redone every time the camera moves (the depth order changes). Modern viewers do this fast enough on a GPU.

Coordinate gotcha. Different pipelines assume different "up" axes. Splatfacto PLYs are +Z-up; Lyra and WorldMirror are +Y-down (OpenCV-style). You fix this with a one-line rotation on the splat mesh in the viewer. Get it wrong and the scene loads upside-down.

5 — Splat → mesh

Splats are great to look at, but they aren't surfaces. Sometimes you need an actual polygon mesh — and getting one is lossy.

A splat is a cloud of overlapping fuzzy blobs. No faces, no edges, no "skin". For most viewing, that's fine. But game engines (Unity, Unreal), physics, collision, and standard 3D tools all expect a mesh. A mesh is also much smaller and renders on any hardware.

The problem: you have to extract a surface from blobs that were never a surface. Main approaches:

MethodIdeaTrade-off
Poissontreat blob centres as an oriented point cloud, fit a watertight surfacesmooth; over-blurs detail
Marching cubessample combined density on a 3D grid, extract isosurfacefaithful; needs a fine grid
TSDF fusionrender depth maps from many views, fuse into a volumegood geometry; needs render step
SuGaR / 2DGStrain the splat to be surface-like in the first placebest meshes; must retrain

The honest catch: it's lossy. Splats can represent fuzzy things — leaves, hair, grass — that genuinely aren't surfaces. A mesh of a garden will always be blobbier than the splat.

The widget below is the garden demo embedded — the dropdown switches between the original splat, the Poisson mesh we extracted from it, and an overlay. Useful for seeing exactly what mesh extraction throws away.

widget · garden: splat vs mesh (live demo embedded)
use the dropdown in the top-right to switch representations. Mesh is 25 MB glb; splat is 134 MB.

The recipe (see garden-mesh-viewer repo): parse the splat → drop low-opacity floaters + huge background blobs → crop to the central region → estimate normals → Poisson depth 11 → keep the largest connected component → transfer per-vertex colour from the nearest splat. The lesson: naive Poisson on the raw splat is a blobby mess; filtering and cropping before reconstruction is the whole game.

6 — Beyond per-scene optimisation

Two newer families that break the "30 minutes per scene, needs many photos" wall: feed-forward (~1 second) and generative (makes up unseen parts).

Feed-forward reconstruction (~1 second)

Train one big neural network on thousands of scenes. At test time, give it a few images and it predicts the Gaussians directly in one forward pass. No per-scene optimization. About one second instead of thirty minutes.

  • Lineage: DUSt3R → MASt3R → VGGT → WorldMirror.
  • It also predicts the camera poses itself — no COLMAP needed.
  • Slightly softer than a fully-optimized splat — it generalizes from training data instead of fitting your specific scene.

The hyworld demo is this — Tencent's WorldMirror 2.0. We swept 14 scenes; each ran in ~1 second on one GPU at 2.35 GB VRAM.

Generative (hallucinates unseen views)

Use a diffusion model to imagine a scene — or the video of moving through it — from a text prompt or one image, including the parts that were never photographed.

  • Lyra 2.0 — one image → diffusion generates an orbit video → gsplat optimization turns the video into a clean splat. The diffusion invents the unseen sides of the scene.
  • HunyuanWorld 2.0 — a full 5-stage pipeline: text/image → panorama → trajectories → photoreal video → splat. See HUNYUANWORLD_PIPELINE.md for stage-by-stage detail.

7 — The landscape & where we fit

Image / prompt → explorable 3D world. The same target World Labs and Marble are chasing. 3DGS is the rendering layer that makes it practical.

Our four demos cover the three families end-to-end:

plantclassical optimisation · 30 min per scene · phone video → splat gardensplat → mesh (Poisson) · for game engines & Blender hyworldfeed-forward · 14 scenes, ~1 second each on one GPU lyragenerative · one image → diffusion video → splat

What's still hard (open problems to speak to)

  • Unbounded scenes — skies and far backgrounds have no clean surface; meshes break here.
  • Dynamic scenes — moving content needs 4DGS (one extra time dimension); active research.
  • Relighting / editing — a baked splat has lighting frozen in; editing it is mostly unsolved.
  • Multi-GPU cost — the full generative pipelines (HunyuanWorld's upper stages) still need clusters.

Glossary (the words you can now use without faking it)

3DGS 3D Gaussian Splatting. Gaussian a fuzzy coloured ellipsoid; the scene primitive. NeRF earlier neural, implicit ("calculator") scene representation. Novel view synthesis rendering a scene from unphotographed angles. COLMAP / SfM recovers camera poses + sparse points from photos. Rasterization / splatting project-sort-blend rendering. Differentiable rendering a renderer that also runs backward. Adaptive density control clone / split / prune during training. Spherical harmonics (SH) encodes view-dependent colour. Anisotropy Gaussians stretching unequally along their 3 axes. Feed-forward reconstruction predict the splat in one network pass. Diffusion model generative model; here, imagines unseen views. Poisson reconstruction fits a watertight mesh to oriented points. .ply / .splat / .ksplat / .spz splat file formats.
